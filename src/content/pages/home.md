My monograph _Music in the Flesh: An Early Modern Musical Physiology_ (Chicago, 2023) reimagines the lived experiences of people making music—composers, performers, listeners—in seventeenth- and early eighteenth-century Europe. 

There are countless historical testimonies describing the powerful effects of music on the early modern body. Music is described as moving, ravishing, painful, dangerous, curative, and miraculous. It affected 'the circulation of the humors, the purification of the blood, the dilation of the vessels and pores.'

What were these early modern European bodies like that music could generate such potent bodily-spiritual effects within them? My book argues that early modern music-making challenges our modern understanding of human nature as a mind-body dichotomy. Instead, it affirms a more integrated vision of human nature, in which body, soul and spirit are inextricably entangled.

The book explores musical, philosophical, anatomical, medical, theological and devotional writings of the time. It uses these writings as a basis for reimagining a 'musical physiology' that is as historically illuminating as it is relevant for present-day performance.
