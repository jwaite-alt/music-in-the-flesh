This event explores some of the material and embodied dimensions of music making in both historical and present-day contexts. In the workshop, led by Stephane Crayton, Anna Brownsted, and Bettina Varwig, we will work with a group of musicians to illuminate these material dimensions from the perspective of both musical production (performance) and reception (listening). We will consider the material affordances of historical bows and strings from the seventeenth to the nineteenth centuries, in conjunction with associated changes in musical sound and performance practice, as well as the concomitant development of the disembodied listening rituals of the modern concert hall. We will then invite participants to experiment with certain multisensory (touch-taste-sound) modes of listening with the whole body, aimed at foregrounding this music’s ever-present but often marginalised materiality. 
 
In the talk, Bettina Varwig will introduce some ideas from her recent book _Music in the Flesh: An Early Modern Musical Physiology_ (Chicago, 2023), which offers a corporeal history of early modern music making. Drawing on theological, anatomical and musical discourses of the time, the talk will show that the remarkable psycho-physiological effects of music on its participants relied on an experience of the human body as porous, volatile, and open to infiltration from music as a quasi-material substance that could redirect a person’s internal humoral-spiritual flows. We will focus in particular on music’s capacity to induce as well as alleviate pain, within an early modern Christian framework of original sin and penitence. The talk will also reflect on a recent performance of J. S. Bach’s _St. John Passion_, in which we set out to bring to life some of the material and embodied affordances of this music for a present-day audience.
