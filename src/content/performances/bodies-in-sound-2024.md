## About this performance

_Bodies in Sound_ was the inaugural performance experiment emerging from the research behind _Music in the Flesh_. The evening brought together early music specialists, historians, and a general audience to explore what it might mean to experience seventeenth-century music on its own physical and spiritual terms.

### Programme

The programme drew on sacred and secular repertoire from the long seventeenth century, including works by Heinrich Schütz, Johann Hermann Schein, and anonymous vocal chamber music from the period. Pieces were chosen specifically for their documented accounts of powerful bodily effects — music described in contemporary sources as moving, ravishing, or curative.

### Approach

Rather than presenting a conventional concert, the evening was structured as a series of experiments. Between pieces, Bettina Varwig offered short reflections on early modern theories of how music acts upon the body — through the circulation of the humours, the dilation of the vessels and pores, the purification of the blood. Audiences were invited to notice their own physical responses.

### Response

Audience feedback was gathered via written responses and an online form. The responses revealed a striking range of bodily and emotional reactions, and provided valuable data for ongoing research into how historical framings of musical experience can shape present-day reception.

> "I found myself listening differently — attending to sensation rather than meaning. Something shifted."
> — Audience member

A full analysis of audience responses is in preparation.
